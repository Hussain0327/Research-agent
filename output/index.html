Introduction to Testing

Software testing is a critical process in the development lifecycle, ensuring that applications meet quality standards and function as intended. Fundamental concepts include the distinction between functional and non-functional testing, as well as testing levels such as unit, integration, system, and acceptance. Each level serves a unique purpose, contributing to the overall reliability of the software.

Practices often span both manual and automated approaches. Manual testing enables exploratory discovery and user-centric insights, while automation enhances efficiency and consistency—especially for regression coverage and critical-path flows. Teams benefit from familiarity with modern tools and frameworks to streamline workflows.

Getting these foundations right helps teams identify defects earlier, maintain requirements alignment, and ship higher-quality software with confidence.

Best Practices for Test Cases

High-quality test cases are clear, concise, and purposeful.

  • Clarity: Use a unique ID, a descriptive title, and unambiguous steps and outcomes.
  • Structure: Include prerequisites, steps, expected results, and actual results in a consistent template.
  • Prioritization: Focus on risk and business impact to cover critical functionality first.
  • Review cadence: Revisit cases as requirements change; use peer reviews to maintain relevance.

Balance depth and coverage. Combine targeted unit tests with integration and E2E checks to validate system behaviour where it matters most.

Challenges in Testing

Ambiguous requirements: Misaligned expectations cascade into defects. Promote tight stakeholder collaboration and living specifications.

Time constraints: Deadlines compress scope. Use automation for stable, repetitive checks and prioritize high-risk paths.

Change velocity: Frequent releases stress reliability. Adopt a robust regression strategy, monitor flake rate, and enforce fast feedback in CI.
